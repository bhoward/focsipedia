Mathematical Induction

(Content adapted from Critchlow & Eck)

When we want to prove quantified statements such as âˆ€xP(x)\forall xP(x)âˆ€xP(x), we can only get so far with the natural deduction rules seen in the previous section. For most results we will need to know more about the structure of the domain of discourse.

The structure of the natural numbersâ€”0, 1, 2, 3, and on to infinityâ€”makes possible a powerful proof technique known as induction or mathematical induction. The idea behind induction is simple. Let PPP be a one-place predicate whose domain of discourse includes the natural numbers. Suppose that we can prove that P(0)P(0)P(0) is true. Suppose that we can also prove the statements P(0)â†’P(1)P(0)\rightarrow P(1)P(0)â†’P(1), P(1)â†’P(2)P(1)\rightarrow P(2)P(1)â†’P(2), P(2)â†’P(3)P(2)\rightarrow P(3)P(2)â†’P(3), and so on. The principal of mathematical induction is the observation that we can then conclude that P(n)P(n)P(n) is true for all natural numbers nnn. This should be clear. Since P(0)P(0)P(0) and P(0)â†’P(1)P(0)\rightarrow P(1)P(0)â†’P(1) are true, we can apply the rule of modus ponens to conclude that P(1)P(1)P(1) is true. Then, since P(1)P(1)P(1) and P(1)â†’P(2)P(1)\rightarrow P(2)P(1)â†’P(2) are true, we can conclude by modus ponens that P(2)P(2)P(2) is true. From P(2)P(2)P(2) and P(2)â†’P(3)P(2)\rightarrow P(3)P(2)â†’P(3), we conclude that P(3)P(3)P(3) is true. For any given nnn in the set N\NN, we can continue this chain of deduction for nnn steps to prove that P(n)P(n)P(n) is true.

When applying induction, we don't actually prove each of the implications P(0)â†’P(1)P(0)\rightarrow P(1)P(0)â†’P(1), P(1)â†’P(2)P(1)\rightarrow P(2)P(1)â†’P(2), and so on, individually. That would require an infinite amount of work. The whole point of induction is to avoid any infinitely long process. Instead, we prove âˆ€kâ€‰(P(k)â†’P(k+1))\forall k\,(P(k)\rightarrow P(k+1))âˆ€k(P(k)â†’P(k+1)) (where the domain of discourse for the predicate PPP is N\NN). The statement âˆ€kâ€‰(P(k)â†’P(k+1))\forall k\,(P(k)\rightarrow P(k+1))âˆ€k(P(k)â†’P(k+1)) summarizes all the infinitely many implications in a single statement. Stated formally, the principle of mathematical induction says that if we can prove the statement P(0)âˆ§(âˆ€kâ€‰(P(k)â†’P(k+1))P(0)\land \big(\forall k\,(P(k)\rightarrow P(k+1)\big)P(0)âˆ§(âˆ€k(P(k)â†’P(k+1)), then we can deduce that âˆ€nâ€‰P(n)\forall n\,P(n)âˆ€nP(n) (again, with N\NN as the domain of discourse).

It should be intuitively clear that the principle of induction is valid. It follows from the fact that the list 0, 1, 2, 3, â€¦, if extended long enough, will eventually include any given natural number. If we start from P(0)P(0)P(0) and take enough steps of the form P(k)â†’P(k+1)P(k)\rightarrow P(k+1)P(k)â†’P(k+1), we can get P(n)P(n)P(n) for any given natural number nnn. However, whenever we deal with infinity, we are courting the possibility of paradox. We can prove the principle of induction rigorously, but for now we just state it as a theorem:

Theorem: Mathematical Induction
Let PPP be a one-place predicate whose domain of discourse includes the natural numbers. Suppose that P(0)âˆ§(âˆ€kâˆˆNâ€‰(P(k)â†’P(k+1)))P(0)\land \big(\forall k\in\N\,(P(k)\rightarrow P(k+1))\big)P(0)âˆ§(âˆ€kâˆˆN(P(k)â†’P(k+1))). Then P(n)P(n)P(n) is true for all natural numbers nnn. Mathematical induction can be applied in many situations: you can prove things about strings of characters by doing induction on the length of the string, things about graphs by doing induction on the number of nodes in the graph, things about grammars by doing induction on the number of productions in the grammar, and so on. An extension known as structural induction will allow us to prove things about data structures such as lists and trees. We'll be looking at applications of induction for the rest of this chapter, and throughout the remainder of the text. Although proofs by induction can be very different from one another, they all follow just a few basic structures. A proof based on the preceding theorem always has two parts. First, P(0)P(0)P(0) is proved. This is called the base case of the induction. Then the statement âˆ€kâ€‰(P(k)â†’P(k+1))\forall k\,(P(k)\rightarrow P(k+1))âˆ€k(P(k)â†’P(k+1)) is proved. This in turn can be proved by assuming that P(k)P(k)P(k) is true and proving that the truth of P(k+1)P(k+1)P(k+1) follows from that assumption. This case is called the inductive case, and P(k)P(k)P(k) is called the inductive hypothesis or the induction hypothesis. Note that the base case is just as important as the inductive case. By itself, the truth of the statement âˆ€kâ€‰(P(k)â†’P(k+1))\forall k\,(P(k)\rightarrow P(k+1))âˆ€k(P(k)â†’P(k+1)) says nothing at all about the truth of any of the individual statements P(n)P(n)P(n). Strong Mathematical Inductionâ€‹

There is a second form of the principle of mathematical induction which is useful in some cases. To apply the first form of induction, we assume P(k)P(k)P(k) for an arbitrary natural number kkk and show that P(k+1)P(k+1)P(k+1) follows from that assumption. In the second form of induction, the assumption is that P(x)P(x)P(x) holds for all xxx between 0 and kkk inclusive, and we show that P(k+1)P(k+1)P(k+1) follows from this. This gives us a lot more to work with when deducing P(k+1)P(k+1)P(k+1). We will need this second form of induction in the next two sections.
